Parkinson's Disease

In Parkinson's disease, certain nerve cells called neurons in the brain gradually break down or die. Many of the symptoms of Parkinson's are due to a loss of neurons that produce a chemical messenger in your brain called dopamine. A decrease in dopamine levels causes irregular brain activity, which leads to the major symptoms of Parkinson’s like issues with movement. Dopamine is produced by nerve cells in the substantial nigra, which is affected by Parkinson’s. This progressive disorder also affects the nerve cells in the basal ganglia, another deeply rooted brain component. Parkinson’s is mostly associated with random tremors and other uncontrollable movements, like shaking, stiffness, and difficulty with balance and coordination.

Issues associated with Parkinson’s - Cognitive problems like thinking difficulties or dementia - Depression or other emotional changes - Chewing problems caused by Parkinson’s in the late stage when the muscles in the mouth are affected - Sleeping problems, like waking up frequently in the night, early, or falling asleep during the day and rapid eye movement sleep behavior disorder that involves acting out dreams - Bladder problems - Constipation - Blood pressure changes - Pain throughout the body - Sexual dysfunction
